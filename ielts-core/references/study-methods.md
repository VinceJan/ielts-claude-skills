# Evidence-Aligned IELTS Study Design

## Principles

- Practice should be diagnostic: every session should identify what failed, why it failed, and what to do next.
- Use active recall and spaced review for vocabulary and reusable language.
- Prefer deliberate review over volume when errors are repeated.
- Use official IELTS samples, Cambridge practice tests, and examiner comments for calibration.
- Keep AI feedback useful but humble: AI is strong at analysis, rewriting, pattern extraction, and recall scheduling; it is weaker as an official score substitute.

## Listening

Effective design:

- Do timed first-pass practice.
- Review wrong answers with the audio script.
- Identify the failure type: lexical gap, weak form/linking, distractor, paraphrase, spelling, speed, or attention drift.
- Repeat short answer-bearing segments before full-section repeats.
- Faster playback can be useful after comprehension is established, but it is not a universal requirement.

Avoid:

- Only passive listening.
- Repeating full audio many times without locating failure points.
- Treating 1.5x comprehension as a guaranteed band target.

## Reading

Effective design:

- Do timed practice first.
- Review by question type and evidence line.
- Build synonym/paraphrase pairs from real questions.
- Track T/F/NG errors separately from matching and completion errors.
- Use deep reading for selected passages, not every passage forever.

Avoid:

- Looking at answers before recording timed attempts.
- Explaining answers without quoting the evidence sentence.

## Writing

Effective design:

- Separate planning, drafting, and correction.
- Score by criterion, with evidence.
- Rewrite only after diagnosing the original.
- Preserve the user's argument when rewriting, unless the argument fails the task.
- Keep a short error ledger: task response, paragraph logic, lexical precision, grammar accuracy.

Avoid:

- Giving a single confident AI band without uncertainty.
- Encouraging memorized template essays.
- Upgrading vocabulary beyond natural control.

## Speaking

Effective design:

- Build reusable personal story banks for Part 2, but train adaptation.
- Use short natural answers for Part 1.
- Train Part 3 reasoning with claim-reason-example-qualification.
- Practice aloud with recording or voice tools; written scripts are preparation, not speaking ability.

Avoid:

- Over-memorized answers.
- Claiming fixed topic coverage from a small story count.

## Vocabulary

Effective design:

- Learn words in context and retrieve them actively.
- Schedule reviews with expanding or adaptive intervals.
- Include meaning, form, collocation, pronunciation if relevant, and one user-relevant sentence.
- Mark words mastered only after repeated successful retrieval.

Evidence note:

- Spaced practice is well supported for long-term retention.
- Research comparing equal and expanding intervals is mixed; expanding intervals are a practical default, not a law.
